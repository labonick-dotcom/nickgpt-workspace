## Golden Commerce (Chris's Business)

**What it is:** A custom WordPress/WooCommerce plugin that functions as a full SaaS platform for precious metals (gold, silver) retailers. He basically built a custom ERP/POS on top of WooCommerce.

**Core functionality:**
- **Dynamic pricing:** Automatically adjusts product prices based on a live spot market feed (10-second updates). Retailers always maintain their target margin regardless of gold/silver price movement.
- **POS integration:** The in-store POS is the "source of truth" -- tracks inventory, cash flow, and strategic business metrics in real-time.
- **WooCommerce base:** Built on WooCommerce intentionally to inherit its plugin ecosystem (eBay, QuickBooks, etc.) without rebuilding integrations from scratch. Same logic as: build on Microsoft and you inherit 100 native integrations.

**Pricing:** $0 upfront, $300/month.

**Current biggest challenge:** Client onboarding. Getting a metals dealer to manually enter their existing inventory is the #1 adoption barrier.

**Scaling strategy:** Already built. Next phase is growing the customer base.

## Strategic Takeaways (Nick & Daniel Business)

### 1. The Ian Model Reframe (Gorski's idea, Daniel loved it)

Right now: Nick and Daniel do project work for Ian's coaching clients as part of Nick's retainer. The current structure is unsustainable -- Nick gets ~3 hours of work for ~$500 per project.

**The pivot:** Go to Ian and say: "This project work we've been doing for your clients -- that's the business we're spinning off. We want to pay you a referral fee for every client we bring in from your audience."

Why it works: Ian will be supportive (he's not adversarial, he's a champion). Framing it as a spin-off with a referral fee is clean, professional, and aligns incentives. Nick and Daniel own the client relationship; Ian gets passive revenue from referrals. Builds their own brand instead of building Ian's.

**Daniel:** "Ian will understand that shit."
**Nick:** "I'm not fighting with him. He's supportive, if anything."

This reframe would also be the pivot moment at the July 9 webinar -- presenting as Nick and Daniel's standalone product rather than an extension of the UYSP offering.

Stop spreading across vending groups, metals dealers, and random one-off websites. **The niche is coaching.** High-ticket buyers ($100K-$350K packages), aligned with existing audience (Ian's 375 members), and directly in Nick and Daniel's wheelhouse.

The "house of cards" problem: doing one-off website projects (vending group, etc.) creates a long tail of one-off maintenance with no scalability. Coaching audience = repeatable, productized, scalable.

**Immediate action:** Build a website specifically for the coaching AI product. Create a clean landing page + Stripe checkout. Ship it. This is a blocking item before real sales.

### 5. The Deal Intelligence Product (Daniel's Vision)

Daniel has been building a prototype in VS Code. Concept: an AI-native deal management interface for enterprise sellers.

**What it looks like:**
- Open it and there's a sidebar with your live deals
- Click into a deal: see all discovery notes aggregated, meeting prep, milestones, timelines, people involved
- An agent on top that tells you the next step in the deal
- All the skills built in: proposal building, RFP responses, meeting prep, follow-up generation
- It's a workspace you run deals from, not a CRM you update

**Nick's visualization:** Deal dashboard where box size scales to deal size. Biggest whale = biggest box, most prominent. Deals that need attention bubble up. Kevin Castro at the bottom in a tiny circle.

**Why it's interesting:** Daniel couldn't get the login flow to work in VS Code. His plan: give the prototype code to Fable and have Fable build out a full working version.

**The competitive framing:** HubSpot is supposed to be simple and generates nightmare stories. There's no AI-native interface where you message it to configure it, make this look better, reorganize by opportunity size. That gap is real.

**Caution:** Enterprise AI CRM is a crowded and capital-intensive space. Not the immediate priority. But the concept for a seller-facing productivity layer -- not a CRM replacement but a working-deals workspace -- is a genuinely differentiated idea worth prototyping.
